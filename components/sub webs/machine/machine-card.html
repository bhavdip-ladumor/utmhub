Injection Molding Machines

This is the heavy hitter of the industry. If you’re looking at a complex, solid plastic part (like a Lego brick, a phone case, or a dashboard), it was likely made here.

How it works: Plastic pellets are melted and "injected" under high pressure into a custom metal mold.
Best for: High-volume production of intricate, identical parts.

Blow Molding Machines

If it’s hollow, it’s probably blow molded. This process is essentially "glass blowing" but for plastic.

How it works: A molten tube of plastic (a parison) is placed inside a mold, and compressed air is blown into it until it expands to fit the shape of the mold.
Best for: Bottles, jugs, fuel tanks, and storage bins.

Extrusion Machines

Think of this as an industrial pasta maker. It creates continuous shapes with a fixed cross-section.

How it works: Melted plastic is pushed through a "die" (a shaped opening) to create a long, continuous strand.
Best for: Pipes, tubing, window frames, straws, and plastic films.

Thermoforming & Vacuum Forming

This process uses plastic sheets rather than raw pellets.

How it works: A flat sheet of plastic is heated until it's soft, then stretched over a mold and held in place by a vacuum.
Best for: Disposable cups, food containers (like clamshells), and refrigerator liners.

Rotational Molding (Rotomolding)

Used for very large, hollow items that need to be sturdy.

How it works: Plastic powder is placed in a mold that rotates on two axes while being heated. The powder coats the entire inside of the mold evenly.
Best for: Kayaks, large water tanks, and playground slides.
